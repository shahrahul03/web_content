The key to creativity in early childhood education is imagination, self-expression and problem-solving. It helps kids learn through doing; they can act out their ideas, learn to think for themselves and build confidence. In the curriculum, creativity enables children to develop their imagination that cuts across the disciplines of art, drama, music and physical activities. By incorporating creativity into these aspects of teaching, educators can create spaces where children flourish, express themselves, and connect to others. This method allows children to explore their imagination openly, enabling them to think outside the box.

Many theories support the belief that creativity is important and crucial. Several social constructivism highlights the role of social interaction in creative development (Hartmann, 2015). A child learns and creates with the help of his peer group and teacher support. This suggests using cultural tools that help to enhance the creative growth of the children, for instance stories or drawing. In a similar vein, other theory of multiple intelligences acknowledges other types of strengths such as spatial intelligences in art or interpersonal intelligence in dramatic play. This allows educators to tailor activities for individual learning styles. According to several constructivist perspective, children learnt through exploration and play and, therefore, it is through play that this creative expression emerges. All theories cited provide weighty scaffolding for the understanding of raising creativity in the early years.

According to the componential theory of creativity, domain-relevant skills, intrinsic motivation, and environment must be combined to generate creativity.

Drama and Puppetry

Importance of Drama and Puppets in Facilitating Creativity
Drama and puppetry foster creativity through role-play and storytelling, which is important for social and emotional development. In imaginary play children narrate stories, develop empathy and solve problems. From different play theories, it has been indicated that engaging in pretend play improves children’s cognitive abilities (Baker, 1996). Through puppets, shy children can express themselves which builds confidence. Through these activities, teachers can develop group dynamics that promote cultural awareness. With this in mind consider drama and puppets as important features in a creative curriculum.

Creativity Theories and Perspectives
According to different sociocultural theory, play encourages creativity because it is done among peers. It is said that preschool children use puppets to enact symbolic plays, though they don’t use them for the same (Karaolis, 2023). The theory of investment by different scholar explain how creativity can be demonstrated by buying low and selling high. They highlight how drama helps develop divergent thinking and problem-solving. Teachers can help students explore different perspectives and create new things to improve their skills.

Critical Analysis

Colour Blocks for 0–2 years engaged children in stacking and colour recognition. The addition of sound elements (tapping blocks together) drew more interest, but some infants lost focus—introducing simple songs about colours could help. Music Math for 3–5 years was an exciting combination of rhythm and counting. Some struggled to keep the beat while drawing; starting with simpler patterns would build confidence. Both activities demonstrated the value of blending disciplines, promoting both creative and analytical thinking.
